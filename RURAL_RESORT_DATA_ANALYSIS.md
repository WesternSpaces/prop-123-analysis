## Aggregate Statistics Across All 13 Counties

### Housing Stock
- **Total Housing Units**: 158,950
- **Occupied Units**: 103,529 (65.1%)
- **Vacant Units**: 55,421 (34.9%)
- **Seasonal/Recreational Units**: 43,894 (27.6% of all housing)

### Critical Finding: The Vacancy Paradox
- **34.9% overall vacancy rate** - nearly 3x the healthy rate of 10-12%
- **79.2% of vacant units are seasonal/recreational** (second homes, short-term rentals)
- **Only 6,221 units vacant for rent or sale** (3.9% of total housing)
- Even with massive vacancy, workers cannot find affordable year-round housing

### The Missing Middle Problem

In most rural resort counties:
- **35-45% of households earn 80% AMI or below**
- **Yet median home values range from $400K to $1.1M**
- **Median rents range from $1,076 to $2,032**

This creates the "missing middle" where:
- People earning 60-100% AMI make "too much" for traditional affordable housing
- But cannot afford market-rate housing in their own communities
- Current Prop 123 limits (60% rental / 100% ownership) exclude most essential workers

## Critical Insights for Prop 123 Analysis

### 1. The Seasonal Housing Problem
- **43,894 units** (27.6% of all housing) are seasonal/recreational
- These units are **unavailable to the workforce** despite high vacancy rates
- Grand, Hinsdale, Lake counties: ~90% of vacant units are seasonal

### 2. Affordability Mismatch
- Counties with **highest median incomes** ($100K+) still have severe affordability crises
- Median home values are **7-11x median household income**
- Even "affordable" units at 120% AMI sit vacant because workers still can't afford them

### 3. The Weighted Average Catch-22
Current petition process requires weighted average AMI calculation:
- Counties have both **very low-income** (30-50% AMI) and **missing middle** (60-140% AMI) needs
- Averaging these together may not show high enough weighted average
- This **prevents access to higher AMI limits** even with documented workforce crisis

### 4. Cost Burden is Severe
- Up to **29.5% of renters** paying >50% of income for housing
- This is **double the HUD definition** of severe cost burden
- Affects all income levels, not just low-income households

### 5. Limited Supply vs. High Vacancy
- **Only 6,221 units** available for rent/sale across all 13 counties
- Yet **55,421 units are vacant** (9x more vacant units than available)
- The problem is not total vacancy, but **availability of year-round workforce housing**

---

## Implications for Prop 123 Policy

### Why Current AMI Limits Fail Rural Resort Counties

1. **60% AMI rental limit** excludes teachers, nurses, service workers
2. **100% AMI ownership limit** excludes most essential workers
3. **Weighted average requirement** creates mathematical barrier
4. **AMI calculation includes** investment income and wealthy retirees, skewing limits upward
5. **Volatile AMI fluctuations** create moving targets (e.g., Routt County: 50% AMI jump 2020-2025)

### Data Supports Policy Recommendations

The data clearly demonstrates:
1. All 12 rural resort counties face **similar structural challenges**
2. Standard AMI limits are **fundamentally mismatched** to workforce housing needs
3. High vacancy rates do **not translate to available workforce housing**
4. Counties with documented crises may **fail weighted average test**
5. Petition process is **unnecessarily burdensome** for small mountain communities
